What is IVCS

The Integrated Village Co-operative Societies (IVCS) are unique home-grown village/community based organizations/ credit cooperative societies initiated by the Cooperation Department, Government of Meghalaya in 2011-12 which are grown and nurtured under the Meghalaya Livelihood and Access to Markets Project (Megha-LAMP). The IVCS are formally recognized as Primary Agricultural Credit Society (PACS) vide Government of Meghalaya, Cooperation Department Notification No.COD.12/2015/69 dated Shillong, the 2nd February, 2021

A total of 330 IVCS formed under Phase-I and PhaseII are presently being supported by the project. In the State of Meghalaya, the formal banking network is not being able to provide access to various financial services to all households in the different villages and the distant parts of the State on account of difficult topography with limited transport and communication infrastructure. Residents of many interior villages have to travel long distances just for basic banking services like making a deposit or withdrawing money from their accounts, let alone availing bank credit and other services. Even in areas where bank branches are present, villagers still find it difficult to avail the services on account of cumbersome procedures of banks, language problems, poor knowledge on financial literacy and require handholding assistance in many areas related to banking services. These factors have impacted adversely on residents in remote villages in their not being able to save whatever small amounts that they can and end up spending the same on unhealthy and unproductive activities/ habits. They visit bank branches, most of which are located far away, only for withdrawal of wages credited to their accounts under MNREGA.

It is against this backdrop that the idea of setting up Integrated Village Cooperative Societies (IVCS) emerged. An IVCS is a community based organization that can legally carry out, amongst others, financial activities and provide financial services to its members. It is a village institution that is run by the villagers themselves. After formation, IVCS are formally registered with the Office of the Registrar of Cooperative Societies and hence, are legal entities.

Important points to be noted on formation of IVCS

An IVCS should ideally be a single village IVCS provided the village has at least 150 to 200 Households. In case of villages with less number of Households, an IVCS can still be formed with adjoining/ neighbouring villages joining together to have 150 to 200 Households.

Each IVCS should strive to cover all Households and have 300 or more Members. The more the Members the better for the viability of the IVCS.

The IVCS are formed and develop in collaboration and close coordination with the Office of the Registrar of Cooperative, Societies, Government of Meghalaya.

It may be shared that performing IVCS have managed to obtain various kinds of support, in terms of materials, funds, infrastructure from Government and Government Agencies, exposure and learning and active linkage with MCAB and other banks. Financial Intermediaries (FIs). Thus it is important for an IVCS to try and develop in such a way that it attracts the attention of the authorities and agencies through its performance.

Managing Committee members and Opinion leaders in the IVCS/ Village should encourage all Members to open savings/deposit accounts and avail of credit facilities from the IVCS which they can get at comparatively and reasonably low rate of interest.

The business hours of the IVCS should be clearly mentioned in the office name-board. The business hours can be made flexible to suit the convenience of the residents / members.

It is has been seen that in some existing IVCS, the progress of the IVCS have suffered immensely owing to following reasons:

1. The Secretary suddenly leaves the IVCS after getting a job or lives in another village/ place after marriage. The selection of the Secretary should be done keeping in mind the above. Or, keeping a second person who would also be familiar with the affairs of the IVCS will be helpful in the event of the Secretary having to leave.

2. There is conflict between the IVCS and the office bearers of the Dorbar Shnong (Village Durbar). It is advised that the Headman of the Village should also be the Chairman of the IVCS.
